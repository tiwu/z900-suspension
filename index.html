Rear Sag Setup

Target: 30-35mm rider sag

Detailed Measurement Procedure

1

Measure Unloaded Height

With bike on paddock stand or center stand, measure from rear axle center to a fixed frame reference point directly above. Record as "Distance A".

2

Measure Loaded Height

With rider in full gear seated normally on bike (both feet on ground), measure same points. Record as "Distance B".

3

Calculate Sag

Rider Sag = Distance A - Distance B

Example: 605mm - 570mm = 35mm sag ‚úì

4

Adjust √ñhlins Remote Preload

  • Too much sag (>35mm): Increase preload (turn clockwise)
  • Too little sag (<30mm): Decrease preload (turn counter-clockwise)
  • Make small adjustments and re-measure

Measurement Tips

  • Use consistent reference points for all measurements
  • Ensure bike is on level ground for loaded measurement
  • Have rider bounce gently to settle suspension before measuring
  • Take multiple measurements and average for accuracy

Front Sag Setup

Target: 30-35mm rider sag

Fork Sag Measurement with Zip Tie Method

1

Prepare Zip Tie Markers

Install zip ties around both fork tubes, pushed down against the dust seals. These will show total fork travel.

2

Measure Unloaded Position

With front wheel off ground, measure from bottom of fork tube to zip tie. Record as "Distance A".

3

Measure Loaded Position

With rider seated normally, measure same distance. Record as "Distance B".

4

Calculate and Adjust

Front Sag = Distance A - Distance B

KYB Preload Adjustment:
  • Use 14mm socket with extension
  • Turn clockwise to increase preload (less sag)
  • Turn counter-clockwise to decrease preload (more sag)
  • Adjust both forks equally to maintain balance
‚öñÔ∏è Balance Check

Always ensure both fork legs have identical preload settings. Uneven preload will cause steering and handling issues.

Rebound Damping Adjustment

What Rebound Damping Controls

Rebound damping controls how quickly the suspension returns to its extended position after compression. Proper rebound prevents oscillation while maintaining tire contact.

Too Soft Rebound

  • Pogo effect (bouncing)
  • Oscillation after bumps
  • General instability
  • Wheel rebounds too quickly

Too Firm Rebound

  • Suspension packs down
  • Harsh ride quality
  • Poor return after compression
  • Reduced ground clearance

Adjustment Procedure (Rear First, Then Front)

1

Rear Rebound Adjustment

Locate adjuster wheel above the shock end eye. Start from a known position and make 1-2 click adjustments.

2

Front Rebound Adjustment

Use screwdriver on top screw of right fork leg. Adjust in same direction as rear for balanced handling.

3

Testing Method

  • Bounce test: Push down on bike and release
  • Should return to position in 1.5-2 oscillations
  • Road test at moderate speeds over bumps
  • Check for stability and comfort
üí° Pro Tip

Start with rear rebound adjustment. The rear suspension affects the bike's attitude and weight transfer, which influences front suspension behavior.

Compression Damping Adjustment

What Compression Damping Controls

Compression damping controls the rate at which the suspension compresses under load. It affects dive under braking, support under acceleration, and overall ride quality.

üìå Z900 SE Note

Only front compression is adjustable on the Z900 SE. The √ñhlins S46 rear shock does not have adjustable compression damping.

Too Soft Compression

  • Excessive brake dive
  • Bottoming on big bumps
  • Vague support feeling
  • Excessive fork travel usage

Too Firm Compression

  • Harsh impact absorption
  • Chatter over small bumps
  • Reduced tire contact
  • Skipping over surface irregularities

Front Compression Adjustment Procedure

1

Locate Adjustment Point

Find the compression damping adjuster at the bottom of each fork leg. May require removing a protective cap.

2

Initial Setting

Start from a known baseline (factory setting or recorded position). Make small adjustments of 1-2 clicks.

3

Road Testing Protocol

  • Test brake dive at moderate speeds
  • Check bottoming resistance over larger bumps
  • Evaluate small bump compliance
  • Assess overall comfort and control
4

Fine Tuning

Adjust based on riding style and conditions. Sport riding may prefer firmer compression, while touring benefits from softer settings.

Testing & Verification Protocols

Systematic Testing Approach

Proper testing ensures your suspension adjustments translate to real-world performance improvements.

üîó Zip-Tie Travel Verification

Setup: Install zip ties on fork tubes and shock shaft

Procedure: Ride normal test loop, check travel usage

Target: Use 80-90% of available travel on bigger bumps

üèçÔ∏è Static Bounce Test

Front: Push down on handlebars, release, observe return

Rear: Push down on seat/tail, release, observe return

Ideal: 1-2 oscillations before settling

üõ£Ô∏è Road Test Protocol

Standard Test Loop Should Include:
  • Smooth straight sections for stability check
  • Moderate cornering for balance assessment
  • Braking zones for dive evaluation
  • Various bump sizes for absorption testing
  • Familiar route for comparison reference

Bracket and Converge Method

1

Find the Range

Test at opposite extremes to identify the usable adjustment range for your riding style.

2

Split the Difference

Adjust to midpoint between extremes and test again.

3

Converge on Optimum

Continue bracketing until you find the sweet spot for your preferences.

üéØ Consistency is Key
  • Test at same tire pressures
  • Use same riding gear/weight
  • Test in similar conditions
  • Allow suspension to warm up
  • Make only one adjustment at a time

Troubleshooting Common Issues

üéØ Can't Achieve Target Sag

If preload is at maximum and sag is still excessive:

  • Spring rate may be too soft for rider weight
  • Consider aftermarket spring upgrade
  • Check for worn suspension components

If preload is at minimum and sag is insufficient:

  • Spring rate may be too firm
  • Consider softer spring option
  • Verify measurement technique

‚öñÔ∏è Settings Are at Extremes

When damping adjustments are maxed out:

  • Spring rate likely incorrect for application
  • Consider complete suspension upgrade
  • Evaluate riding style vs. suspension setup
  • Check for internal suspension damage

üîß Hardware Issues

When adjusters don't work properly:

  • Clean adjustment points of dirt/corrosion
  • Use proper tools to avoid damage
  • Check for worn or damaged adjusters
  • Consider professional service if binding occurs

üèçÔ∏è Handling Problems Persist

When suspension feels correct but handling is poor:

  • Check tire pressures and condition
  • Verify wheel alignment
  • Inspect steering head bearings
  • Consider chassis geometry issues

Hardware Upgrade Considerations

When to Consider Spring Changes

  • Can't achieve proper sag range
  • Damping adjustments at extremes
  • Significant weight change (rider + gear)
  • Dramatic riding style change

When to Seek Professional Help

  • Internal suspension component failure
  • Oil leaks from dampers
  • Binding or damaged adjusters
  • Persistent handling issues after proper setup
